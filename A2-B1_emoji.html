Reading

Emojis speak louder than words 📢

Read the text carefully, then answer the questions below.

Every day, people send over 10 billion emojis — more than there are people on Earth. But here's the surprising part: two people can look at the exact same emoji and understand completely different things.

Take 🙂. In the UK and the US, many young people use it to show they are not happy — it often means "I'm fine" in a passive-aggressive way, the same way someone says "fine" when they clearly aren't. But in many other countries, it simply means "I'm smiling."

The thumbs-up 👍 is perfectly friendly in most of the world. But in some cultures, it can actually be rude. And 🙃 (the upside-down face) looks like a smile, but most English speakers use it to say "this is a disaster and I'm smiling to cope."

So emojis aren't a universal language — they're more like dialects. Just as English has different words and meanings in Australia, the UK, and the US, emojis carry different meanings depending on who sends them and where they're from.

Grammar Focus

-ed vs. -ing adjectives 🎯

Study the grammar box, then do the quick check below.

📚 Grammar Box

Use -ing adjectives to describe a thing, situation, or person that causes the feeling.

The emoji conversation was confusing. (= it caused confusion)
Riley's emotions in Inside Out 2 are overwhelming. (= they overwhelm her)

Use -ed adjectives to describe how a person feels.

I was confused by the emoji. (= I felt confusion)
She felt overwhelmed by her new emotions. (= she had that feeling)
Common pairs to remember:

boring / bored  ·  confusing / confused  ·  exciting / excited

surprising / surprised  ·  frustrating / frustrated  ·  amusing / amused

fascinating / fascinated  ·  embarrassing / embarrassed
